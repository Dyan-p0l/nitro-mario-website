    Nitrogen Cycle

    Nitrogen is an incredibly adaptable element, existing in both organic and inorganic forms as well as many different oxidation states. The movement of nitrogen between the atmosphere, biosphere, and geosphere in different forms is called the nitrogen cycle, one of the major biogeochemical cycles.

    Nitrogen Cycle is a biogeochemical process through which nitrogen is converted into many forms, consecutively passing from the atmosphere to the soil to organism and back into the atmosphere.

    Process of the Nitrogen Cycle consists of the following steps â€“ Nitrogen fixation, Assimilation, Ammonification, Nitrification and Dentrification.

    Atmospheric nitrogen (N2) constitutes about 78% of the Earth's atmosphere but is not directly usable by most organisms. Nitrogen fixation is the conversion of atmospheric nitrogen into biologically available forms like ammonia (NH3) or nitrate (NO3-).

    Nitrogen assimilation refers to the process by which plants and other organisms take up and incorporate nitrogen into organic molecules, such as proteins, nucleic acids, and chlorophyll. Nitrogen is an essential nutrient for all living organisms, as it is a major component of amino acids, which are the building blocks of proteins.

Later, the produced nitrites are converted into nitrates by Nitrobacter. This conversion is very important as ammonia gas is toxic for plants

    Denitrification is the process of returning nitrogen compounds to the environment by transforming nitrate (NO3-) into gaseous nitrogen. The final stage of the nitrogen cycle happens in the absence of oxygen. Denitrification is carried out by the denitrifying bacterial species Clostridium and Pseudomonas, which consume nitrate to obtain oxygen and produce free nitrogen gas as a byproduct.
